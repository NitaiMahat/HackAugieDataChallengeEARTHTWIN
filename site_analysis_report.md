## The Pattern — East vs. West

There is a clear geographic split in the data:

- **Bottom 5 (lowest ozone, 36–38 ppb):** All Eastern US — New York, North Carolina,
  Virginia, Connecticut, Pennsylvania. These are rural, forested, and relatively protected areas.

- **Top 5 (highest ozone, 48–52 ppb):** All Western US — Colorado, Wyoming, California.
  High elevation, but crucially, all near areas with significant industrial and construction activity.

**The elevation alone does not explain this.** VPI120 (Blue Grass Trail, VA) sits at 652 m and
reads 37 ppb. GTH161 (Gothic, CO) is at 2,915 m and reads 50 ppb. Elevation can contribute to
ozone but the human activity around these western sites is the differentiating factor.

---

## Why Construction and Industrial Activity Is Part of This

### Pinedale, WY (PND165) — 51 ppb
Pinedale sits directly adjacent to the **Jonah Field and Pinedale Anticline** — one of the
most active natural gas extraction regions in the US. Drilling rigs, compressor stations, and
heavy equipment fleets generate VOCs and NOx at scale. These are the same emission types
produced by construction sites. Wyoming's state environmental agency has flagged this region
for ozone non-attainment precisely because of energy extraction activity.

### Rocky Mountain National Park, CO (ROM206) — 48 ppb
RMNP itself is protected, but it sits **downwind of the Denver–Boulder metro corridor** —
one of the fastest-growing construction markets in the US. Front Range development (new
housing, highway expansion, commercial builds) pumps emissions that travel west into the
park on prevailing winds. The EPA has documented this transport effect.

### Gothic / Gunnison County, CO (GTH161) — 50 ppb
Gunnison County has seen consistent growth in tourism infrastructure construction (ski-adjacent
development, resort expansion, road upgrades). The Rocky Mountain Biological Laboratory
that hosts this site is surrounded by a development corridor that has expanded steadily
since 2018.

### Centennial, WY (CNT169) — 51 ppb
Near Laramie, WY — a region with active oil and gas operations and proximity to
construction corridors along I-80. One of the highest-elevation CASTNET sites in the
network, yet also one of the highest ozone readings.

### La Posta, CA (LPO010) — 52 ppb ← HIGHEST
La Posta is a tribal monitoring site in eastern San Diego County. This area sits in the
transport path of **ozone plumes from the San Diego metro** — a region experiencing a
sustained construction boom (residential development, infrastructure, military base expansion).
Eastern San Diego County regularly receives transported urban pollution on afternoon sea breezes.
La Posta recording the highest ozone in the entire dataset is consistent with this pattern.

## What This Means for Earth Twin

These are not industrial sites. They are **rural monitoring stations** — the kind of places
designed to measure background air quality away from direct emission sources. The fact that
they are recording ozone levels of 48–52 ppb tells us something important:

> **Construction and industrial emissions do not stay where they start.**
> They travel. They accumulate. And they show up in places that had nothing to do with
> the activity that caused them.

This is the core argument for a pre-construction planning tool:

- If a project in Denver pushes 8% more ozone into the airshed, that ozone ends up
  being measured at Rocky Mountain National Park.
- If Pinedale's gas fields ran smarter scheduling and lower-emission equipment,
  the site's 51 ppb average would be measurably lower.
- The communities and ecosystems downwind pay the cost of decisions made upwind.

Earth Twin gives planners the ability to see that cost *before* the project starts —
not after it shows up at a monitoring station 50 miles away.
